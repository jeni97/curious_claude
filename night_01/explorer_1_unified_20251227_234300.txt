Step 3: How is the boiling point measured without relying on the Celsius scale?

We find that temperature can be measured in absolute scales (Kelvin) or by other means (e.g., thermodynamic principles). The boiling point of water is 373.15 K.

But how is Kelvin defined? The Kelvin scale is defined by setting the triple point of water at 273.16 K and absolute zero at 0 K. The triple point is a specific condition (temperature and pressure) where water exists in equilibrium as solid, liquid, and gas.

Step 4: Now we push on the triple point. How do we know the triple point of water is exactly 273.16 K?
Search: "how is the triple point of water determined"

We find that the triple point is a fixed point used to calibrate thermometers. It is defined by international agreement (International System of Units, SI) and is maintained in metrology labs using triple point cells.

But how do we know that every triple point cell is the same? We assume that water is pure and the conditions are controlled. However, we must consider:

- Isotopic composition: Water with different isotopes (like heavy water) has a different triple point. The SI definition specifies the isotopic composition (Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water).

We find it's a standard established by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) to represent the average isotopic composition of ocean water. But how was this average determined? By sampling ocean water from various locations.

Now we push: How do we know that the sampling is representative? What if the ocean changes? And what about the fact that the standard is an artificial construct?

Step 6: We are now at a point where the verification relies on a standard set by an international body. We can question the authority of the body.
